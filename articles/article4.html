Taleb- A contrarian

 

Posted on 16th August, 2020

In an ironic turn of events and fortunes, when the financial Crisis of 2008 was destroying the biggies of the financial world, Nassim Taleb's fortunes shot upward. He was vindicated, yet again. 

Just the last year, Taleb had published his second book, "The Black Swan" which focussed on rare events that normally lie outside the realms of our expectations and as and when they happen, cause big devastations. The book went on to become a bestseller. It has been described as one of the 12 most influential books since World War II. Interestingly, Taleb thinks of his book’s success as yet another example of a Black Swan event.

 

Nassim Nocholas Taleb was born in Amioun, Lebanon on March 27, 1960, to parents, Minerva Ghosn and Nagib Taleb. Nagib Taleb was an oncologist and researcher in anthropology. He did his bachelors and masters from the University of Paris and then came to the US to do MBA from Wharton Business School at the University of Pennsylvania. It's another thing that he doesn't have a high regard for the degree now. Taleb also holds a PhD in management science from the University of Paris.

 

He started his career as a trader, worked as a quantitative analyst and ran his own investment firm, and the more he studied statistics, the more he became convinced that the entire financial system was a keg of dynamite that was ready to blow.

Taleb made his first big killing on the day of October 19, 1987, now commonly referred to as Black Monday. He had accumulated a large position in near-worthless out-of-the-money Eurodollar futures puts going against the norm. On that Monday, it paid off big time. But he attributed his winnings to luck.

In 2003, Nassim Taleb predicted a high probability of the government-sponsored institution Fannie Mae collapse. While Fannie Mae was considered a highly trusted expert on risk management in the mortgage market, Taleb analyzed its risks and gave an extremely negative outlook. He compared it to sitting on a barrel of dynamite vulnerable to the slightest hiccup. This forecast caused a wave of outrage and a lot of attacks in the financial world, but finally came true. Eventually, the collapse of Fannie Mae cost the US taxpayers hundreds of billions of dollars.

 

In the aftermath of the 2008 housing markets collapse and the ensuing financial crisis, Taleb became famous. These events were later recognized as Black Swans.

Taleb is usually described as an arrogant person who always criticizes everyone. But I believe, he does it right. Many seemingly intelligent and successful people are the products of positive luck, so it only makes sense not to completely credit them with their fortunes or fame.

 

In an interview with Bloomberg’s Erik Schatzker in 2018, he said, "The world is more fragile today than it was in 2007." Today's economy suffers from the “same disease” as before. The meltdown in 2007 was a “crisis of debt,” and if anything, the problem has only worsened. Maybe the debt is not at the same place, but it's there. And today, the covid 19 pandemic has shown how the world has become really very fragile and stressed with debt and dysfunctional systems.

So, if we really want to know about Taleb, we need to read his books and assimilate the learnings at all levels. Individually, we can reorganize our lives to counter sudden shocks. On a macro level, corporations and governments would do well to embrace his recommendations about becoming antifragile.
